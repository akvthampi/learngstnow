Chapter 11: Accounts and Records

Understanding GST Record Keeping Requirements

← Previous Chapter: Tax Invoice, Credit Notes and Debit Notes Next Chapter: E-Way Bill →

Introduction to Accounts and Records

Accounts and records form the foundation of GST compliance and are essential for accurate tax reporting, audit requirements, and verification by tax authorities. Proper maintenance of books of accounts ensures transparency and facilitates smooth business operations under the GST regime.

Definition and Importance

Accounts and records under GST refer to the systematic documentation of all financial transactions, tax-related activities, and business operations that enable accurate computation of tax liability, input tax credit claims, and compliance with statutory requirements. They serve as evidence during audits and inspections.

Legal Framework

Account and record requirements are governed by Sections 37 to 44 of the CGST Act, 2017:

  • Section 37: Accounts and records to be kept by registered person
  • Section 38: Furnishing details of outward supplies
  • Section 39: Furnishing details of inward supplies
  • Section 40: Furnishing information regarding imports
  • Section 41: Furnishing information regarding exports
  • Section 42: Matching, reversal, and reclaim of input tax credit
  • Section 43: Accounts and records to be kept by Input Service Distributor
  • Section 44: Audit

Key Points to Remember

  • Mandatory records for all registered persons
  • Records must be maintained for 6 years
  • Can be kept in electronic format
  • Proper indexing and categorization required
  • Must be readily accessible for inspection

Mandatory Records for Registered Persons

Registered persons must maintain the following records:

  • Production or manufacture of goods
  • Inward and outward supply of goods or services
  • Stock of goods
  • Input tax credit availed
  • Output tax payable and paid
  • Goods or services supplied on behalf of principals
  • Accounts of purchases, sales, income, and expenditure
  • Tax invoices, credit notes, debit notes, and other documents

Types of Records

Financial Records

Books of accounts including cash book, ledger, journal, and trial balance showing all financial transactions related to business operations.

Tax Records

Records of tax invoices, credit/debit notes, tax payments, ITC claims, and GST returns filed with tax authorities.

Inventory Records

Stock registers, purchase registers, sales registers, and records of goods sent for job work or received from job workers.

Supporting Documents

Contracts, agreements, correspondence, and other documents supporting business transactions and tax computations.

Record Keeping Requirements

  • True and Correct: Records must accurately reflect business transactions
  • Systematic Manner: Proper classification and indexing required
  • Regular Updates: Records must be maintained on a regular basis
  • Accessibility: Must be readily available for inspection
  • Retention: Maintain for 6 years from end of financial year
  • Language: Can be maintained in English or regional language

Special Records for Different Business Types

Business Type Special Record Requirements
Manufacturers Production records, consumption registers, and manufacturing accounts
Traders Purchase and sales registers, stock registers, and delivery challans
Service Providers Service registers, client-wise records, and service completion certificates
E-commerce Operators Supplier details, transaction records, and TCS collection records
Input Service Distributors Distribution registers and allocation records

Electronic Record Keeping

Records can be maintained in electronic format with specific requirements:

  • Proper indexing and search capabilities
  • Regular backups and data security
  • Readily producible in physical form if required
  • Compatible with GST portal requirements
  • Proper access controls and audit trails

Record Keeping Scenarios

  • Manufacturer: Maintains production records, raw material consumption, finished goods stock, and job work records.
  • Trader: Keeps purchase register, sales register, stock register, and delivery challans for all transactions.
  • Service Provider: Maintains service register, client-wise records, and supporting documents for services rendered.
  • E-commerce Operator: Keeps supplier database, transaction records, and TCS collection details.

Job Work Related Records

Special records required for job work arrangements:

  • Records of goods sent for job work
  • Records of goods received back from job worker
  • Job work agreements and contracts
  • Challans for goods movement
  • Payment records to job workers

Input Service Distributor Records

ISD must maintain specific records:

  • Records of input services received
  • Distribution registers
  • Allocation basis documentation
  • Recipient-wise distribution details
  • Supporting calculation sheets

Audit and Inspection Requirements

Records must be prepared for audit and inspection:

  • Ready accessibility during audit
  • Proper documentation for all transactions
  • Supporting evidence for tax computations
  • Reconciliation of books with returns
  • Proper authorization and approval records

Common Record Keeping Issues

Businesses commonly face these record keeping challenges:

  • Inadequate record maintenance systems
  • Improper classification of transactions
  • Lack of proper indexing and retrieval systems
  • Insufficient backup and data security
  • Non-compliance with retention requirements

Recent Amendments and Updates

The GST Council has made several updates to record keeping requirements:

  • Simplified record keeping for small businesses
  • Enhanced electronic record capabilities
  • Special provisions for e-commerce records
  • Improved audit trail requirements
  • Streamlined job work documentation

Conclusion

Proper maintenance of accounts and records is fundamental to GST compliance and business transparency. Understanding the mandatory requirements, maintaining systematic records, and ensuring proper documentation help businesses avoid compliance issues and facilitate smooth operations. Regular training on record keeping requirements, implementation of appropriate systems, and periodic reviews ensure that businesses meet all statutory obligations. Well-maintained records not only help in audit preparedness but also enable better business decision-making through accurate financial information.
