Chapter 2: Supply under GST

Understanding the Concept and Types of Supply under GST Law

← Previous Chapter: Introduction to GST Next Chapter: Charge of GST →

Concept of Supply

The term "supply" is the most fundamental concept in GST law. It is the foundation on which the entire GST structure is built. Under GST, almost all transactions involving goods and services are considered as supply and are therefore liable to tax.

Definition of Supply

As per Section 7 of the CGST Act, 2017, supply includes all forms of supply of goods and/or services such as sale, transfer, barter, exchange, license, rental, lease or disposal made or agreed to be made for a consideration by a person in the course or furtherance of business.

Essential Elements of Supply

For a transaction to qualify as supply under GST, it must satisfy the following essential elements:

  • Supply of Goods and/or Services: The transaction must involve either goods, services, or both
  • For Consideration: There must be a consideration (payment) involved, unless specifically exempted
  • In the Course or Furtherance of Business: The activity must be related to business operations
  • Made by a Taxable Person: The supplier must be a registered person under GST
  • In the Taxable Territory: The supply must take place within the taxable territory of India

Types of Supply

Supply under GST can be broadly classified into various categories based on different criteria:

Taxable Supply

Supplies that are chargeable to GST at the applicable rates. These include most commercial transactions of goods and services.

Exempt Supply

Supplies that are either nil rated or wholly exempt from GST. No tax is payable on such supplies.

Zero Rated Supply

Supplies on which no tax is payable but input tax credit can be claimed. Includes exports and supplies to SEZ.

Classification Based on Nature

1. Goods Supply

Supply involving tangible movable property. Examples include sale of products, machinery, vehicles, etc.

2. Services Supply

Supply involving intangible benefits or facilities. Examples include consultancy, transportation, banking services, etc.

3. Composite Supply

A supply consisting of two or more taxable supplies of goods or services which are naturally bundled and supplied in conjunction with each other. One of these supplies is the principal supply.

4. Mixed Supply

Two or more individual supplies of goods or services made in conjunction with each other by a taxable person for a single price where such supply does not constitute a composite supply.

Classification Based on Territory

Intra-State Supply

Supply where the location of the supplier and the place of supply are in the same state or union territory. Subject to CGST and SGST/UTGST.

Inter-State Supply

Supply where the location of the supplier and the place of supply are in different states or union territories. Subject to IGST.

Important Points to Remember

  • Supply is the core concept of GST law
  • All supplies are taxable unless specifically exempted
  • Consideration is generally required for a transaction to be considered as supply
  • Business activities are the primary focus of GST supply provisions
  • Different tax treatments apply to intra-state and inter-state supplies
  • Composite and mixed supplies have different tax implications

Activities Deemed as Supply

Certain activities are specifically deemed as supply even in the absence of consideration:

  • Permanent transfer or disposal of business assets where input tax credit has been availed
  • Supply of goods or services between related persons
  • Supply of goods or services by any agent who is authorized to do so
  • Import of services for consideration whether or not in the course or furtherance of business
  • Activities specified in Schedule I of the CGST Act

Activities Not Treated as Supply

Certain activities are specifically excluded from the definition of supply:

  • Services by an employee to the employer in the course of employment
  • Services of funeral, burial, crematorium or mortuary
  • Sale of land (not buildings)
  • Transactions specified in Schedule III of the CGST Act
  • Activities undertaken by the government as public authority

Comparison: Composite vs Mixed Supply

Aspect Composite Supply Mixed Supply
Definition Naturally bundled supplies Individually distinct supplies
Relationship Supplies are naturally connected Supplies are independent
Tax Treatment Tax rate of principal supply applies Highest tax rate among supplies applies
Example AC with installation service Gift pack with different items

Time of Supply

The time when a supply is deemed to have taken place is crucial for determining the liability to pay GST:

  • For goods: Earlier of receipt of payment or issue of invoice
  • For services: Earlier of receipt of payment or completion of service
  • Special rules apply for continuous supply of services

Conclusion

Understanding the concept of supply is fundamental to mastering GST. It determines what transactions are taxable, the applicable tax rates, and the time when tax liability arises. Businesses must carefully analyze their transactions to determine if they constitute supply under GST and apply the appropriate tax treatment. The classification of supplies into various categories helps in proper compliance and ensures that the right amount of tax is collected and remitted to the government.
